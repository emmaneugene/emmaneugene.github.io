about me:|

Hello! My name is Emmanuel Oh.

I am a senior year Computer Science student with a second major in Public Policy at Singapore Management University.

I love solving problems, understanding how things work through tinkering, and recombining that knowledge to create new solutions. I believe that my skills in software, DIY and working with people put me in good stead to contribute towards solving some of the wicked problems of the present. I am especially interested in building reliable software that serves the public good and/or developing solutions to mitigate climate change. At the same time, I'm very aware that there are a lot of things I don't know yet, and I want to build technical expertise by networking with the brigtest people in the field and working in as many high-impact roles as I can.

I am an impatient optimist. I believe that creating a sustainable and equitable future is well within our means, and I want to see it happen in my lifetime. I see challenges as opportunities for problem-solving and avenues for us to exercise our collective ingenuity and innate capacity for change - and I believe that those are the traits required for any complex system to flourish.

The bulk of my experience lies mainly with low-level and systems programming languages like C and Java, but I am also fluent with higher-level languages like Python and JavaScript. I have experience working with relational and document databases, deploying to cloud provid.ers (AWS, DigitalOcean, Google Cloud) and DevOps tooling (Docker, Gitlab CI/CD, CloudFormation, Kubernetes). My medium-term goal is to be a site reliability engineer with a deep understanding of low-level systems and hardware, so that I can build and maintain software that handles critical production loads on bare-metal infrastructure. In the longer term, I hope to be able to implement these same concepts for managing robots and automation at scale.

Career aspirations aside, I make it a point to carve out time for volunteering, personal projects, and engaging in varied hobbies. I love learning new things about the world and interacting with people who are passionate about their craft. I use social media to share snippets of my life, hobbies, and personal recommendations - and I would love to connect if you have any interests in common. I'm also dabbling with content creation - writing is and will always be my mainstay, but I'm looking into videos and podcasts in the future as well.

Inspired by Den Delimarsky's 'How I work'. Since work is becoming more flexible, multi-threaded, and project-based, I think it's useful to outline my own principles, habits and expectations when working with others.

This document will be updated occasionally, but I will primarily be adding content.

Principles

Initiative

I don't like waiting around for things to get done, or being blocked by excessively bureaucratic processes. I would sooner find my way around an obstacle than continue facing the same problem on a regular basis. In an increasingly creator-driven economy, initiative pays off dividends.

Honesty

Honesty is one of my strongest values. I prefer honest criticism to false praise, and I accord the same treatment to others.

Fast feedback

Progress, learning and improvement are all built on the foundation of feedback. I seek out good feedback as frequently as I can, ideally from those who are much more experienced than me.

Perfect is the enemy of good

It's better to create something scrappy than to hold off on your work because you feel like it isn't good enough. The momentum and feedback you get from starting something will trump any potential downsides.

Push work, pull updates.

I think the push-pull model used in software development is the most effective way of enabling productivity in a way that feels natural and comfortable. You push work to a common source according to your own pace, and keep updated with what others have done on the same platform. There is no unnecessary back-and-forth, and people can work at their own pace in a non-blocking manner. I strongly believe that this model is equally effective across all fields of creative work, and I try to implement in as many areas of my life as possible.

Principles before rules

I don't believe in blindly following rules and regulations unless I know why they were implemented. I believe that all rules are derived from intentions and first principles. If you can understand and respect those principles, that counts for more.

Fix the mundane problems first

As much as we celebrate high-key achievements, it's the daily processes that determine whether we succeed or fail as individuals, organizations and communities. Without good habits and processes in place, we simply can't speak of accomplishing the bigger goals. The work that goes unnoticed and taken for granted is what makes the most impact. Make the everyday chores effortless, then aim for greater.

Always happy to be proven wrong

I never assume that my knowledge and understanding of anything is complete, and I'm always striving to educate myself further - so that I may become less ignorant and more appreciative of the things we take for granted. I don't see any shame in being proven wrong, and I'm thankful for these opportunities for learning.

Assume the best of others

In a word: trust. It's difficult to build, but the process of building strong relationships always starts with that foundation. Most of the time, I'm comfortable with putting my faith in the people I work with, and I don't rush to attribute mistakes or wrongdoings to malice. It's nothing complicated, but it's helped me a lot in my relationships with others.

Don't hold grudges

It's impossible to go through life without encountering any misunderstandings or conflicts, especially in the context of work. I try my best not to take these things personally and make an effort to make amends wherever I can. Even if relationships go sour, I don't harp on past mistakes, and I try to be civil to the best of my ability.

Habits

  • - I usually work from mornings (~8am) to the late afternoon (~5pm), and leave time outside for creative pursuits. I find that I get the best work done in the early mornings, so I try to minimize unnecessary communication during this period.

  • - I like batching work together, and I often assign specific days of the week to work on different projects or topics. I would prefer work arrangements that enable me to achieve this level of flexibility.

  • - I generally prefer physical meetups and face-to-face communciation when coming up with new ideas, but I'm happy to use informal, asynchronous modes like online chats for minor clarifications.

  • - That being said, I highly appreciate due preparation before any meetings or scheduled appointments. It would great to send a primer or agenda for longer meetings.

  • - I strongly dislike busywork and excessive back-and-forth communication. The more things can be automated, information can be organized, and complex work can be itemized (such as on lists and kanbans), the better.

  • - I don't expect instantaneous replies, but I would prefer receiving a response within 1 working day if possible. if you're busy and can't reply properly at the moment, I'd really appreciate a heads-up.

ideas:|

Some of broad-themed questions and problems I'm mulling over - I'm hoping to tackle at least a few of them once I've accumulated enough knowledge, resources and social connections:

Electronic waste: Technology is enabling us to do much more than we were able to before, but there's one major issue - we haven't been able to close the loop with broken devices. There are many ways to mitigate this problem - by far, the easiest would be making hardware more modular and easy to repair, but beyond that, we urgently need to find methods to recycle the materials from broken hardware in a cost-efficient manner.

Public health: Implementing policies that target the low-hanging fruit and make the most impact for population-level health outcomes - I'm thinking of things like noise and air pollution reduction, expanding green spaces (see: solarpunk), nutrition, sleep and pre-emptive health interventions.

Cognition: It's no secret that many of the wicked problems we're facing will only be solved with human ingenuity. How do we give ourselves the best shot at those outcomes? I'm increasingly interested in the fields of cognitive health, neuroscience and (dare I say it) biohacking. I think there's a wealth of interesting ways you can experiment with the elements of your daily routine - and there's really no better time to try it than the present.

Robotics: I believe that human progress is defined by our ability to solve complex problems - from the very earliest days, we built tools to help us accomplish tasks with less effort and time - thereby allowing us to do even more with our limited time and energy. To me, intelligent automation marks the next step of this endeavour. I think intelligent, nature-inspired robots will be crucial for us to flourish in a changing world, and I'm really keen to learn more about the field.

Energy: Energy powers everything that we do. It's the foundation of modern society and the crucial challenge for us is to provide clean, reliable energy to meet the needs of humanity as soon as possible. Clean energy sources like solar and wind are, in many cases, already cheaper than fossil fuels, but the challenge is how to store that energy for future use. Beyond new battery technologies, I'm also interested in exploring nuclear power (which I feel has been sorely overlooked) - the future of energy is something that I'll continue to keep my eye on.
