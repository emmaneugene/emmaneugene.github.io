about me:|

Hello! My name is Emmanuel. I've gone by many nicknames, but 'Emman' usually works.

I am currently in my final year reading Computer Science at Singapore Management University.

I love solving problems, asking hard questions, and tinkering. I'm looking to use my skills to work in high-impact roles tackling vital engineering problems in an open, collaborative context.

I am an impatient optimist. I believe that creating a sustainable and equitable future is well within our means, and I want to see it happen in my lifetime. However, I know there's still a lot for me to learn, and I'd like to develop my expertise by working with the brightest people in the field.

The bulk of my interest and experience lies in backend development and system administration. I enjoy working with low-level languages like C and Java, but I am fluent with higher-level languages like Python and JavaScript as well. I have experience working with relational and document databases, cloud deployments, DevOps workflows, CI tooling and system design. As part of my course specialization in AI, I also have experience implementing data science workflows and machine learning models.

Broadly, my career focus is on understanding low-level concepts and working with systems in order to build and maintain software that handles critical production loads. I'm actively looking out for roles that will help me build these skillsets (e.g. backend development, DevOps and SRE). I also believe in learning in public. Education, advocacy and community engagement are vitally important to me, and I actively share my knowledge through teaching and creating educational content online.

Some of my interests include strength training, nutrition and health science, model kits, anime, jazz, and technical deep dives.

work:|

Inspired by Den Delimarsky's 'How I work'. Since work is becoming more flexible, asynchronous and project-based, I think it's useful to outline my own principles and expectations when working with others.

  • - I usually work from mornings to the late afternoon (8am - 5pm), and leave time outside for creative pursuits. I find that I get the best work done in the morning, so I try to minimize communication during this period.

  • - I like batching work together, and I often assign specific days of the week to work on a specific project or topic.

  • - I generally prefer physical or face-to-face communication for first meetings and discussing ideas, but updates and clarifications can be done asynchronously on chats/task boards.

  • - I strive to avoid repetitive back-and-forth. This means I often use pins, documentation, or calendar events for important information. The more comprehensive, the better.

  • - I don't expect instantaneous replies, but I would prefer receiving a response within 1 working day (which is a standard I hold myself to as well). Otherwise, a heads-up would be much appreciated.

  • - Ask me questions! Give me feedback! I love input of any kind, as long as it's constructive. As a rule of thumb, I believe every single decision is open to criticism.

ideas:|

Some of broad-themed questions and problems I'm mulling over - I'm hoping to work on at least a few of them once I've accumulated enough knowledge, resources and social connections:

Electronic Waste: Technology is enabling us to do much more than we were able to before, but there's one major issue - we haven't been able to close the loop with broken devices. There are many ways to mitigate this problem - by far, the easiest would be making hardware more modular and easy to repair, but beyond that, we urgently need to find methods to recycle the materials from broken hardware in a cost-efficient manner.

Public health: Implementing policies that target the low-hanging fruit and make the most impact for community-level health outcomes - I'm thinking of things like reducing noise and air pollution, expanding green spaces (see: solarpunk), nutrition, sleep and pre-emptive health interventions.

Cognition: There are many wicked problems in the world, and the truth is that they will only be solved by human ingenuity. How do we give ourselves the best shot at those outcomes? I'm increasingly interested in the fields of cognitive health, neuroscience and (dare I say it) biohacking. I think there's a wealth of interesting ways you can experiment with better living just by starting with the elements of your daily routine - and there's really no better time to try it than the present.

Robotics: I believe that human progress is defined by our ability to solve complex problems - from the very earliest days, we built tools to help us accomplish tasks with less effort and time - thereby allowing us to do even more with our limited time and energy. To me, intelligent automation marks the next step of this endeavour. We're probably a long way away, but I believe that nature-inspired robots will be crucial for us to flourish in a changing world, and I'm really keen to learn more.

Energy: Energy powers everything that we do. It's the foundation of modern society and our biggest challenge is to provide clean, reliable sources to meet humanity's needs as soon as possible. Technologies like solar and wind are in many cases already cheaper than fossil fuels, but we desperately need innovation in batteries to solve the problem of intermittency. I also think that nuclear power is a promising alternative that could play a much bigger role.

Mental Health: Interest in mental health is growing at a stronger pace than ever, spurred by anxieties about the future, the breakneck pace of modern life, and the erosion of traditional communities and civic bonds. How do we prepare people - young and old - to thrive in this environment? How can we design policies, institutions and systems to be more humane? There are no easy answers when it comes to this, but if there's anything I know for sure, it's that change can only begin once we're able to have these conversations openly.
