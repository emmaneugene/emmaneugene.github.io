about me:|

Hello! My name is Emmanuel Oh.

I am a senior year Computer Science student wih a second major in Public Policy at Singapore Management University.

I love solving problems, understanding how things work by taking them apart, and recombining that knowledge to create new ideas. I believe that my skills in software, DIY and working with people put me in good stead to make a dent in solving some of the wicked problems we face. I am especially interested in building reliable software that serves the public good or tackles pertinent issues relating to climate change.

The bulk of my experience lies mainly with low-level and systems programming languages like C and Java, but I am also fluent with higher-level languages like Python and JavaScript. I have experience working with relational and document databases, deploying to cloud providers (AWS, DigitalOcean, Google Cloud) and DevOps tooling (Docker, Gitlab, CloudFormation, Kubernetes). My long-term goal is to be a site reliability engineer with a deep understanding of low-level systems and hardware, so that I can build and maintain software that handles critical production loads on bare-metal infrastructure.

Career aspirations aside, I make it a point to carve out time for volunteering, personal projects, and engaging in varied hobbies. I love learning new things about the world and interacting with people who are passionate about their craft. I use social media to share my snippets of my life, hobbies, and personal recommendations - and I would love to connect if you have any interests in common. I'm also dabbling with content creation - writing is and will always be my mainstay, but I'm looking into videos and podcasts in the future as well.

now:|

A brief summary of what's going for me at the moment:

  • - Embarking on my final year (or rather 1.5 years) of studies at Singapore Management University. I'm planning to graduate in December 2023, and my current focus is on clearing modules for my AI electives and second major in Public Policy. Apart from that, I want to devote my effort into low-level systems programming. I'm revisiting the first (and currently still my favorite) langauge I learnt - C, but in the context of software vulnerabilities and exploits relating to memory and buffer overflow exploits.

  • - Volunteering as a team lead at Ground-Up Initiative (GUI) on the weekends, and I'm looking to play a more active role in building software to streamline workflows and help the organization retain and train volunteers. GUI has big ambitions for the future, and I believe that good software is integral to achieving that. If you'd like to get involved, please let me know!

  • - Learning in public through personal endeavours like writing on Substack, streaming my coding sessions on YouTube Live, and sharing my interests on social media. I have a list of software projects I want to finish by the end of the year - a disk usage visualizer with electron.js and D3.js, and a general feed integrator (RSS and Twitter, but with support for further media down the line) using a full-stack - most likely MERN.

  • - This is a bit of a nascent idea right now, but I'm exploring ideas to embark on a research programme for a coming semester at school, and I'm increasingly interested in the integration of theory and practice in low-level programming. I'm currently exploring the safety limitations and vulnerabilities associated with compiled languages, and I also came across this interesting post about the problem areas for compiled languages that have not adequately been addressed yet. At the same time, I'm very keen to learn more about Rust, and I've heard some very good things about its compiler and inbuilt provisions for safety. I'd like to delve into this space by learning the language and understanding some of the work that's been done with the RustBelt project, and see if I'm able to contribute a little bit to this body of research.

ideas:|

Some of broad-themed questions and problems I'm mulling over - I'm hoping to tackle at least a few of them once I've accumulated enough knowledge, resources and social connections:

  • - Repair culture: Making electronics much easier to repair, closing the loop with e-waste recycling, reducing the embodied energy cost of manufacturing hardware.

  • - Public health: Implementing policies that target the low-hanging fruit and make the most impact for population-level health outcomes - I'm thinking of the reduction of noise and air pollution, expanding green spaces (solarpunk), nutrition, sleep and preventative or pre-emptive health interventions.

  • - Cognition: It's no secret that many of the wicked problems we're facing will only be solved with human ingenuity. How do we give ourselves the best shot at those outcomes? I'm increasingly interested in the field of neuroscience and (dare I say it) biohacking. I think there's a wealth of interesting ways you can experiment with the elements of your daily routine - and there's no better time to try it.
